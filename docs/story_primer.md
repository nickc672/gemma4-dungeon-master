# Harbor of Broken Mornings

## 1. Premise and Tone
The story is set in a small harbor town trapped in a cycle of fear, missing time, and unexplained bloodshed. Every dawn brings new stains and new denials. People have injuries, missing possessions, and altered routines, but few can remember the night clearly enough to explain what happened. The player arrives as an outsider investigator, stepping into a town where everyone is tired, suspicious, and only partially reliable.

The intended tone is grounded mystery with slow-burn dread rather than nonstop combat. The emotional center of the story is not "who did it" in a simple murder-mystery sense, but "what repeated harm has done to this community" and "what moral cost is acceptable to stop it."

Core themes:
- Memory versus truth
- Protection versus control
- Mercy versus justice
- Public panic versus private damage

## 2. Situation at Start
At game start, the player is in **Town Square** at dusk.

The public facts are:
- Bloodstains appear around town with no confirmed missing persons.
- Many residents have missing-hour episodes.
- The town watch and town hall are managing panic, not solving the root problem.
- Rumors blame the reclusive wizard.

The hidden reality at start is:
- Mitch is involved in the violence during dissociative episodes he cannot fully remember.
- The wizard has been using memory intervention to reduce mass panic and keep order.
- Those interventions reduce immediate chaos but increase long-term confusion and mistrust.

This means both key suspects contain partial truth, partial denial, and partial self-justification.

## 3. Structural Story Arc (Intended Progression)
The authored beat flow is designed to support player freedom while preserving a coherent escalation.

### Beat 1: Uneasy Arrival
Objective: establish mood, stakes, and uncertainty.

Expected player experience:
- Observe an anxious public hub.
- Hear repeated but inconsistent rumors.
- Get first leads without being forced into one route.

### Beat 2: Mitch as Urgent Hook
Objective: give the player a personal, emotional lead.

Expected player experience:
- Mitch asks for help and blames the wizard.
- Mitch appears frightened and sincere, but details shift under pressure.
- Player sees tension between sympathy and suspicion.

### Beat 3: Free Investigation Across Districts
Objective: turn the map into a clue web.

Expected player experience:
- Visit civic, dockside, temple, alley, tavern, and watch locations.
- Compare witness reports and physical clues.
- Discover that many contradictions are patterned rather than random.

### Beat 4: Contradictions Become Pattern
Objective: transform rumor into a model.

Expected player experience:
- Multiple records and witnesses imply repeated nighttime cycles.
- Signs of cleanup, altered reports, and recurring injuries appear.
- Player can form hypotheses before the confrontation.

### Beat 5: Direct Confrontation Setup
Objective: force competing narratives into one scene.

Expected player experience:
- Bring gathered evidence to either Mitch, the wizard, or both.
- Notice that both are protecting some truth and hiding some harm.
- Shift from "what happened" to "what should be done now."

### Beat 6: Core Reveal
Objective: clarify causal chain and moral stakes.

Canonical reveal:
- Mitch commits killings in dissociative cycles.
- The wizard repeatedly suppresses/repairs memory traces to prevent social collapse.
- The town is surviving through harmful triage, not healing.

### Beat 7: Resolution by Player Doctrine
Objective: let player values determine outcome.

Valid resolution vectors:
- Punitive justice (prioritize accountability)
- Protective containment (prioritize short-term stability)
- Restorative attempt (riskier, morally demanding compromise)
- Independent intervention (player-defined method)

## 4. World Geography and Investigation Logic
The map is built around a central hub with district spokes and layered interiors.

### Primary Hub
**Town Square**
- Social pressure cooker
- Fast access to official, sacred, market, and watch spaces
- Best place for baseline rumor checks

### Civic Cluster
**Town Hall**, **Watch Barracks**
- Official records and institutional behavior
- Useful for contradictions in logs, statements, and policy

### Sacred Cluster
**Temple of the Tide**, **Old Shrine**, **Old Well**
- Memory, omen, ritual, and confession signals
- Good for emotional truth and recurring-symbol clues

### Harbor Cluster
**Harbor Gate**, **Docks**, **Warehouse Row**, **Fishermen's Shacks**, **Riverside Path**, **South Bridge**
- Physical evidence, movement patterns, and missing-hour traces
- Good for timeline reconstruction and material clues

### Social/Underworld Cluster
**Copper Cup**, **Bar Counter**, **Back Door - Copper Cup**, **Stair Landing**, **Storeroom Door**, **East Alley**, **Smuggler's Entrance**
- Informal intelligence, hidden access, and non-official narratives
- Good for motive pressure and covert logistics

### Restricted Arcane Site
**Wizard's House**
- Endgame context location
- High-value explanation space, but trust-gated

## 5. Key Characters and Their Narrative Jobs
### Mitch
Narrative function: emotional entry point and central contradiction.

What he provides:
- urgency, fear, and eyewitness claims
- clues with unstable detail
- a human face for trauma and harm

What to watch:
- repeated blame patterns
- timeline drift
- physical signs inconsistent with his conscious account

### Town Wizard Arlen
Narrative function: high-control counter-theory and moral foil.

What he provides:
- structural explanation of memory interventions
- practical knowledge of the cycle
- admission of ethically dangerous methods

What to watch:
- paternalistic framing
- selective disclosure
- outcome-driven ethics

### Captain Varr / Watch
Narrative function: institutional stress test.

What they provide:
- patrol records
- enforcement perspective
- pressure from public order demands

What to watch:
- edited or delayed reporting
- priority on calm over truth

### Cleric Serah / Temple
Narrative function: community care lens.

What they provide:
- confessional patterns
- recurring symptom profiles
- moral framing beyond legal framing

What to watch:
- who seeks healing repeatedly
- which details repeat despite memory gaps

### Mara / Copper Cup / Market Voices
Narrative function: ground truth from daily life.

What they provide:
- practical details no institution tracks
- social behavior changes over time
- links between public rumor and private fear

What to watch:
- mundane inconsistencies (codes, shifts, routines)
- who appears where before dawn

## 6. Clue Ecology (What Clues Are Supposed to Do)
Clues are intentionally distributed across social, physical, and administrative channels. Coherence comes from **cross-confirmation**.

Types of clue:
- Physical traces: rope fibers, knife placement, lantern glass, blood-cleaning signs
- Administrative traces: patrol logs, incident packets, duplicate records
- Ritual/arcane traces: prayer beads, chalk fragment, memory vial implications
- Behavioral traces: contradictory testimony, repeated routes, denial patterns

Expected clue logic:
- No single clue solves the story.
- Each major hypothesis should require at least two clue classes.
- Contradictions are not noise; they are evidence of memory intervention and dissociation.

## 7. Truth Model (Canonical Internal Logic)
The story should remain coherent under this model:

1. Nighttime violence occurs.
2. Mitch is a primary agent of that violence during altered states.
3. The wizard intervenes after the fact to prevent panic and retaliatory collapse.
4. Interventions reduce immediate chaos but generate cumulative social distortion.
5. Institutions adapt by partial concealment, selective reporting, and tactical denial.
6. Community trust degrades, creating the exact paranoia the interventions were meant to avoid.

This is the central tragedy: each actor is partly trying to protect the town while also making the long-term wound worse.

## 8. Resolution Philosophy
The ending should not imply a single objectively correct answer. It should reflect the player's governing principle.

Possible player doctrines:
- "Truth at all costs"
- "Stability first"
- "Mercy for the broken"
- "Accountability with reform"

Success criteria for any ending:
- The player made an informed choice using evidence.
- The consequences map cleanly to their doctrine.
- NPC reactions reflect established motivations.

## 9. Practical QA Checklist (for Playtests)
Use this to verify whether a run tracks with intended context.

- Player can freely investigate multiple districts before confrontation.
- Mitch is sympathetic but inconsistent, not cartoonishly evil from turn one.
- Wizard explanation is plausible but morally costly, not pure villain monologue.
- At least three independent clue channels are available before reveal.
- Contradictions resolve into a single causal model by endgame.
- Final choice feels like ethics under uncertainty, not a binary "good vs bad" prompt.

## 10. What Should Be Happening, in One Paragraph
A frightened town is being held together by improvised, morally compromised systems: a traumatized killer who does not fully know himself, a wizard who suppresses memory to prevent collapse, and institutions that prefer controlled ambiguity over panic. The player is meant to move from rumor to pattern to responsibility, then decide which cost the town should bear next.
